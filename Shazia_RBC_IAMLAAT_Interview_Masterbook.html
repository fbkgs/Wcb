    Known fact versus assumption

    Known: the same Senior Manager interviewed Shazia previously. Unknown: the date, questions, answers, feedback and reason she was not selected. Therefore, this strategy focuses on visible growth and consistency without fabricating a post-mortem.

    The strategic objective

    The second interview should feel like a stronger second data point, not a rerun. The Senior Manager already knows Shazia has AML experience. This time, she must make three improvements visible: sharper triage judgment, more specific evidence, and clearer Manager-level ownership.

    Should she mention the previous interview proactively?

    Yes, briefly, in the greeting or early motivation answer. One sentence is enough. It removes awkwardness, demonstrates confidence and makes continued interest look deliberate. She should not launch into why she was not hired or ask the Senior Manager to explain the old decision.

    Recommended acknowledgement

    “It is good to speak with you again. I appreciated our previous conversation about the team, and it reinforced my interest in the close-or-escalate judgment this role requires. Since then, I have been deliberate about strengthening [true area], so I am glad to have the opportunity to speak again.”

    Five-part same-interviewer plan

    1. Preserve the spine of the story

    Keep chronology, core motivation and factual examples consistent. A changed emphasis is fine; changed facts require a transparent explanation.

    2. Upgrade specificity

    Add the exact alert trigger, client profile, transaction pattern, amount/range, lookback, decision, authority and outcome. The second interview must contain evidence the first may have lacked.

    3. Show visible learning

    Describe one or two genuine changes in method: hypothesis-driven reviews, stronger closure rationales, better time-boxing, improved coaching, broader international exposure or clearer executive communication.

    4. Demonstrate calibration

    Give both a strong escalation and a strong closure. Explain what would reverse each decision.

    5. Stay calm under comparison

    A neutral expression is not negative feedback. Answer the question, tolerate pauses and let the Senior Manager probe. Do not fill silence with extra claims.

    Situation-specific language

    Situation Recommended response
    Asked what changed “The biggest change is [true development]. I now start with the decision question, identify the facts that would change the outcome, and keep the review proportionate. That has made my conclusions clearer and my handoffs more useful.”
    Asked about a previously weak answer “I remember that question. My answer then was not as specific as it should have been. A better example is…” Then give a true example with decision and result.
    Challenged on inconsistency “That is a fair question. The underlying fact has not changed; what has changed is how I frame the decision. Previously I emphasized [X]. Today I would add [Y] because…”
    Asked why she was not selected “I was not part of the decision, so I would not speculate. I did reflect on where I could be more concise and specific, and I have strengthened [true area].”
    Interviewer seems neutral Maintain normal pace and tone. Do not ask whether an answer was satisfactory. Treat follow-ups as an invitation to show judgment.
    Prior answer is remembered verbatim Use the same facts but a fresher structure: decision first, evidence second, learning third.
    No direct feedback was provided Say exactly that. Then describe self-assessment, not invented feedback.
    Growth is modest Do not manufacture a promotion. Growth can be better judgment, broader exposure, stronger process discipline or clearer influence.

    What not to do

    • “I know I did not interview well last time.” This sounds defensive unless supported by direct feedback.

    • “I have changed completely.” The Senior Manager may remember the same work history.

    • “You already know my background.” She still has to answer fully and professionally.

    • “I hope you give me a chance this time.” Continued interest should sound deliberate, not pleading.

    • Overfamiliarity, jokes about the prior rejection or references to private details from the last conversation.

    • Word-for-word repetition of memorized answers. Consistency of facts matters; identical phrasing does not.

    4.1 Role and team understanding

    Interview-ready explanation of IAMLAAT

    “IAMLAAT appears to sit between automated or internally referred unusual activity and full AML investigation. Its job is to validate the alert, assess the client and transaction context, decide whether the matter is reasonably explained or warrants deeper investigation, and document that decision in a form that is consistent, timely and defensible. It is not simply a forwarding function. It protects the bank both by preventing meaningful risk from being missed and by preventing weak, poorly scoped escalations from consuming investigative capacity.”

    Alert analysis versus full investigation

    Alert analysis / triage Full AML investigation
    Answers a bounded decision: close, clarify or escalate. Expands scope to related clients, accounts, networks, predicate-risk context, regulatory and control implications.
    Starts from a specific scenario, alert or internal referral. May combine multiple alerts, referrals, intelligence sources and business lines.
    Reviews enough history and context to support the disposition. Performs broader lookbacks, network analysis and corroboration as required.
    Documents why deeper investigation is or is not warranted. Develops the full investigative conclusion and may support STR or other control decisions.
    Should not become an unlimited search for certainty. May require more extensive information gathering and stakeholder engagement.

    Why an alert is not evidence of suspicious activity

    An alert says that activity crossed a rule, model threshold or behavioural expectation. It is a prompt for assessment. Legitimate life events, business growth, seasonal activity, family remittances, treasury operations, data quality or stale KYC can all produce alerts. The analyst’s job is to connect facts, context and indicators—not to treat the trigger as a conclusion.

    Close, escalate and document

    Close: The activity is affirmatively and credibly explained by the client profile, history, funding, counterparties and purpose, with no unresolved material combination of indicators.

    Clarify: One specific, obtainable fact would materially change the decision and no policy or live-risk issue requires immediate escalation. Whether IAMLAAT itself can request information depends on RBC workflow.

    Escalate: The combined unresolved mismatch, fund flow, counterparty, network, intelligence, typology or control concern warrants deeper investigation. Escalation is not an accusation or proof of crime.

    Document: Record the decision, relevant evidence, explanations considered, unresolved issues and next step so another trained reviewer could understand and reproduce the reasoning.

    What makes an escalation useful

    • A one-sentence decision and risk hypothesis.

    • The relevant client profile and expected activity.

    • The material amounts, dates, instruments, inflows, outflows, velocity and retained balance.

    • The key counterparties, jurisdictions and relationships.

    • The combined indicators and why plausible explanations did not resolve them.

    • Prior alerts, linked accounts, adverse information or control concerns that materially change the interpretation.

    • Targeted direction: what the investigator should assess next, not a vague “review further.”

    • Clear separation of confirmed facts, reasonable observations, assumptions and unresolved questions.

    Risk from poor alert decisions

    Failure Potential consequence
    Under-escalation Missed ML/TF/sanctions-evasion or fraud risk; delayed reporting; customer harm; enforcement, remediation and reputational exposure.
    Over-escalation Investigator backlog, slower response to higher-risk matters, inconsistent thresholds and unnecessary cost.
    Weak closure narrative A correct decision may still be indefensible to QA, audit or regulators.
    Weak escalation narrative Investigators repeat work, miss the real risk or cannot identify the intended scope.
    Inconsistent decisioning Similar facts receive different outcomes, weakening control credibility.
    Late decisioning Aging alerts, missed service levels, delayed control action and regulatory risk.

    4.2 End-to-end AML lifecycle

    1. Customer onboarding and KYC: Identify and verify the client; understand occupation/business, ownership/control, purpose, expected products, volume, counterparties, geographies and source of funds/wealth where relevant.

    2. Customer risk rating: Assess inherent and residual risk using factors such as client type, products, geography, delivery channels, ownership, PEP exposure and expected activity.

    3. Ongoing monitoring: Keep customer information current and assess whether activity remains consistent with what is known about the client and risk profile.

    4. Transaction-monitoring scenarios: Rules, thresholds, models or behavioural analytics identify activity requiring review.

    5. Alert generation: The system or internal referral creates a review item. It is not a finding of suspicious activity.

    6. Alert triage / IAMLAAT: Validate the trigger, review client context and relevant activity, consider explanations, and close, clarify or escalate with a defensible rationale.

    7. AML investigation: Conduct deeper analysis of linked parties, broader history, network, predicate-risk context, intelligence and regulatory/control implications.

    8. Regulatory decision: The appropriate authorized function determines whether the RGS threshold for an STR is met and ensures reporting as soon as practicable after the measures establish it.

    9. Control action: Where appropriate, sanctions, fraud, account restriction, KYC remediation, relationship review or other risk processes proceed under policy.

    10. Quality assurance: Review decision accuracy, rationale, policy compliance, consistency, timeliness and data completeness.

    11. Recordkeeping: Preserve the evidence, decision and audit trail required by law and policy.

    12. Regulatory examination / audit: Demonstrate that controls identify, assess, escalate, report and retain records effectively and consistently.

    Where IAMLAAT sits

    IAMLAAT is likely the decision gate between alert generation/internal UTR activity and full AML investigation. It should not claim final ownership of every STR, sanctions or account decision unless RBC has delegated that authority to the role.

    4.3 Canadian AML/ATF regulatory knowledge

    Practical framework

    Canada’s AML/ATF framework is anchored in the Proceeds of Crime (Money Laundering) and Terrorist Financing Act, its regulations and FINTRAC guidance. For interview purposes, Shazia should explain obligations operationally: know the client, assess risk, monitor relationships and transactions, maintain records, identify and assess suspicious activity, report where the legal threshold is met, and maintain an effective compliance program.

    FINTRAC’s role

    FINTRAC is Canada’s financial intelligence unit and AML/ATF supervisor. It receives prescribed reports, assesses compliance and produces financial intelligence disclosures within its statutory mandate. The interview answer should not imply that FINTRAC investigates or prosecutes crimes itself.

    Reasonable grounds to suspect

    Interview-safe definition

    “RGS is above a hunch or simple suspicion and below reasonable grounds to believe. It requires an objective combination of facts, context and indicators supporting the possibility that a transaction or attempted transaction relates to money laundering, terrorist financing or sanctions evasion. Proof is not required, there is no monetary threshold, and the rationale must be articulable to another trained reviewer.”

    Facts, context and indicators

    Facts: Known events or attributes: dates, amounts, transaction types, client details, account history, known orders or confirmed links.

    Context: Information that changes interpretation: occupation, business model, life event, expected corridor, industry practice, historical pattern or customer capacity.

    Indicators: Features associated with ML/TF or sanctions evasion: structuring, rapid depletion, unexplained third parties, opaque ownership, unusual routing or identity evasion. Indicators are clues, not automatic conclusions.

    KYC, beneficial ownership and ongoing monitoring

    KYC is not a one-time form. It establishes the baseline against which observed behaviour is assessed. For entities, beneficial ownership, directors, signing authorities, related companies and control matter because apparently unrelated payments may be connected. Stale or incomplete KYC may require remediation, but it does not by itself prove transactional suspicion; Shazia should assess both the data gap and the activity.

    Sanctions, listed property and high-risk geographies

    A potential sanctions match requires identity resolution and the designated specialist/control process. Shazia should distinguish a potential name match, a confirmed identity match, ownership/control concerns and transactional indicators of sanctions evasion. She should also distinguish an STR for suspected sanctions evasion from separate listed-person or entity property reporting obligations. High-risk geography is context, never the entire rationale.

    Legal-certainty cautions

    • Use “generally,” “under current FINTRAC guidance,” or “subject to RBC policy and delegated authority” where operational details may vary.

    • Do not state that every UAR or UTR is an STR.

    • Do not say a particular fact automatically meets RGS; explain the combined picture.

    • Do not claim to determine criminal guilt, sanctions ownership/control or legal reportability outside her authority.

    • Do not reveal or imply internal thresholds, scenarios or client information that she is not permitted to discuss.

    4.4 Alert-analysis methodology

    A strong analyst starts with the alert hypothesis, not a hunt for every possible issue. The review expands only when the observed risk, policy or information gap justifies it.

    Understand the trigger: What rule, threshold, model or UTR concern generated the review? Is the data accurate?

    Establish the client baseline: Identity, occupation/business, beneficial ownership, purpose, expected volume, products, geography, source of funds and history.

    Map the activity: Amount, frequency, velocity, direction, instruments, cash, wires, e-transfers, currencies, inflows, outflows and retained balance.

    Assess change over time: Is this new, increasing, episodic, seasonal or a continuation of a documented pattern?

    Analyze counterparties: Who funds or receives funds? Are relationships known, plausible, linked or opaque?

    Assess geography and payment chain: Why this corridor, intermediary or jurisdiction? Does the client have credible ties?

    Assess products and channels: Cash, cards, wires, trade finance, correspondent payments, MSBs, virtual currency, online channels and access patterns.

    Test typologies: Structuring, layering, funnel, pass-through, rapid movement, third-party activity, mule behaviour, fraud proceeds, TF, sanctions evasion and trade anomalies.

    Test legitimate explanations: Identify the strongest innocent explanation and the evidence needed to validate it.

    Review prior concerns: Prior alerts, UARs, cases, adverse information, KYC remediation and linked accounts.

    Identify material gaps: Separate facts needed for the disposition from facts that are merely interesting.

    Decide and document: Close, clarify or escalate; state what would change the decision; separate fact, observation, assumption and conclusion.

    4.5 Escalation versus closure

    Factors that support escalation

    • Material mismatch with occupation, business model, financial capacity or historical activity.

    • Unexplained aggregation, rapid depletion, pass-through movement or minimal retained balance.

    • Multiple unrelated third parties, opaque relationships or shared control across accounts.

    • Transactions with no apparent economic purpose after reasonable review.

    • Concerning network links, prior related alerts, fraud complaints, law-enforcement information or credible adverse intelligence.

    • Trade pricing, goods, routes, documents or payer relationships that do not reconcile commercially.

    • Potential TF, sanctions, listed-entity, vulnerable-person or live-risk concerns requiring specialist handling.

    • Client explanation is inconsistent, contradicted by transaction evidence or unsupported where support should exist.

    • Policy-mandated escalation or scope beyond delegated authority.

    Factors that support closure

    • Activity aligns with documented client profile and financial capacity.

    • Source and use of funds are known and credible.

    • Counterparties and relationships are established and plausible.

    • Pattern is consistent with historical behaviour or a documented life/business event.

    • Apparent red flags are affirmatively resolved by objective evidence—not merely absence of adverse media.

    • No unresolved material combination of indicators remains after a proportionate review.

    Unusual but not sufficiently suspicious

    A client may conduct a one-time high-value international transfer, receive a sudden bonus, sell property, expand a business or send money to family in a higher-risk jurisdiction. The activity is unusual because it departs from the baseline, but it may be closed when the source, purpose, beneficiary, capacity and history support a legitimate explanation and no concerning fund-flow or third-party pattern remains.

    Contradictory evidence and incomplete information

    Rank the evidence by reliability and materiality. Identify the specific contradiction. Ask whether a targeted fact can resolve it in time. If the gap is central and creates material opacity, document it and escalate. Do not average conflicting evidence into a vague “some concern” conclusion.

    When to consult the Senior Manager

    • The matter exceeds delegated authority or has significant legal, regulatory or reputational exposure.

    • There is a potential sanctions/listed-entity or immediate fraud/vulnerable-person control issue.

    • The client or matter is sensitive, high-profile or presents conflict-of-interest concerns.

    • Policy language is genuinely ambiguous or comparable cases are being treated inconsistently.

    • The decision could reveal a systemic scenario, data or workflow defect.

    • A high-risk alert is approaching SLA because of an unresolved blocker.

    Avoiding confirmation bias

    • Write the most plausible legitimate explanation before deciding.

    • Ask what fact would make you close and what fact would make you escalate.

    • Separate client/jurisdiction risk from transaction evidence.

    • Review disconfirming evidence, not just supporting indicators.

    • Use consistent lookbacks and decision criteria for similar cases.

    • Invite a focused peer or manager challenge on the disputed fact, not a general “does this feel suspicious?” discussion.

Rapid victim-to-crypto movement, mule patterns, unexplained wealth or high-risk service exposure.

Nature of business, invoices, tax/KYC information, counterparties and volume. Opaque third parties, high-risk products, pass-through or deliberate evasion of business controls.

    4.7 Report writing and rationale

    The five-part rationale

    1. Decision first: close, clarify or escalate.

    2. Profile and trigger: who the client is, expected activity and why the alert fired.

    3. Material activity: amounts, period, flow, counterparties, geography and pattern.

    4. Analysis: why explanations resolve or fail to resolve the indicators.

    5. Action: conclusion and targeted next step.

    Strong closure narrative

    Example

    Close with no escalation. The long-standing client receives regular payroll income and has an established history of supporting immediate family in Pakistan. During the 12-month review, the client sent monthly transfers to the same two beneficiaries and one larger medical-expense payment funded from accumulated salary savings. The beneficiaries and family relationships were corroborated, and no unexplained third-party funding, cash structuring, rapid pass-through activity or new adverse information was identified. The activity is consistent with the client’s profile, financial capacity and historical behaviour.

    Weak closure narrative

    5. Alert-analysis decision framework

    On this page

    2. How to apply it in a case interview
    3. The decision ladder
    4. Fact–observation–assumption–conclusion discipline

    The FACTS framework

    Letter Decision question Evidence
    F — Fit Does the activity fit the client? Identity, occupation/business, expected products, volume, geography, source of funds, ownership and history.
    A — Activity What actually happened? Amounts, dates, instruments, inflows, outflows, velocity, sequencing, cash, currencies and retained balance.
    C — Counterparties & context Who is involved and what changes the interpretation? Relationships, locations, identifiers, payment purpose, business model, life event, internal/external information.
    T — Typologies & triggers Which risk patterns or control concerns are present? Structuring, funnel, pass-through, layering, third parties, fraud, TF, sanctions, trade or policy triggers.
    S — Suspicion & support What is the proportionate disposition? Close when affirmatively explained; clarify when one material fact can decide; escalate when unresolved combined risk warrants deeper review.

    How to apply it in a case interview

    1. Pause and ask one or two clarifying questions if the decision depends on missing scope, client type or policy authority.

    2. State the likely disposition early: “Based on these facts, I would likely escalate, subject to…”

    3. Walk through FACTS in natural language; do not announce every letter unless helpful.

    4. Name the strongest legitimate explanation and why it succeeds or fails.

    5. State the one fact that would change the decision.

    6. End with the documentation and downstream direction.

    The decision ladder

    Close

    The activity is affirmatively explained. The rationale identifies the evidence that resolves the trigger and why no material concerns remain.

    Clarify

    One narrow, obtainable fact can decide the matter, risk is stable and the workflow permits information gathering. The request is targeted and time-bound.

    Escalate

    The combined unresolved risk warrants deeper review, a live/specialist control is triggered, policy requires escalation or the scope exceeds delegated authority.

    Fact–observation–assumption–conclusion discipline

    Category Example
    Fact The account received 94 e-transfers totalling $146,000 in six weeks.
    Observation Most funds were sent abroad within 48 hours and less than 2% remained.
    Assumption to avoid The senders were fraud victims.
    Defensible conclusion The dispersed third-party funding and rapid cross-border depletion are inconsistent with the client profile and warrant investigation for funnel/mule or remittance risk.

    Likely questions: Sensitive case; confidentiality; high-value client; pressure

    Concise STAR-C: “The matter involved a sensitive or high-profile client, but I applied the same evidence standard and restricted information to need-to-know participants. I documented facts separately from assumptions, resisted pressure for a preferred outcome and raised [decision/control issue] through the appropriate channel. The result was [true outcome].”

    Detailed version to build: Explain sensitivity without identifying the client. Describe how access, discussion and documentation were limited, what pressure existed, the objective evidence and who made the final decision.

    Likely follow-ups: Who pressured you?; Would you handle a VIP differently?; How did you protect confidentiality?; Did seniority alter the risk appetite?; What if the business objected?

    How to answer case questions

    State assumptions, give the likely disposition early, use FACTS, test the strongest legitimate explanation, identify what would change the decision and end with the documentation or specialist handoff. RBC policy may alter the operational step.

    Case 1 — Profile-aligned international family remittances

    1. Facts

    • Long-standing personal client employed as an engineer receives regular payroll.

    • Sends $1,500–$2,500 monthly to the same two beneficiaries in Pakistan.

    • A one-time $12,000 wire triggers an aggregate international-transfer scenario.

    • KYC documents close family ties and prior notes show family support. Memo states medical expenses.

    • Funds came from accumulated salary savings; no unexplained cash or third-party funding.

    2. Main risks

    • Higher-risk or monitored corridor

    • One-time increase in value

    3. Questions Shazia should ask

    • Is the larger beneficiary one of the established family beneficiaries?

    • Does the client have the financial capacity?

    • Any new screening/adverse information or prior related alerts?

    4. Additional information to review

    • 12-month inflows/outflows and retained balance

    • Beneficiary relationship and prior transfer pattern

    • Source of funds and any policy-required supporting context

    5. Likely disposition

    Likely close once the beneficiary/relationship and source are confirmed.

    6. Model verbal answer

    ““I would likely close. The alert reflects a threshold breach, but the activity is salary-funded, consistent with documented family ties and a long-standing remittance pattern. I would validate the larger beneficiary, financial capacity and recent screening. Assuming those facts hold and there is no third-party funding or pass-through behaviour, I would document the affirmative explanation. An unrelated beneficiary, fragmented funding or inconsistent purpose would change my decision.””

    7. Common mistakes to avoid

    • Escalating because of geography alone

    • Relying only on the payment memo

    • Saying “no adverse media” is enough

    Case 2 — Newly incorporated consulting company with rapid growth

    1. Facts

    • Expected monthly receipts were $25,000. Within six months, the company receives $180,000–$250,000 monthly from several foreign companies.

    • Most funds are paid within three to five days to contractors in Canada and Europe.

    • No cash, no adverse information and corporate registration appears valid.

    • KYC does not describe the new contracts or counterparties.

    2. Main risks

    • Material profile deviation

    • Rapid onward movement and low retained balance

    • International third parties

    3. Questions Shazia should ask

    • What services and contracts explain the growth?

    • Who are the foreign payers and contractors?

    • Is beneficial ownership current?

    • Do invoices reconcile to amounts and timing?

    4. Additional information to review

    • Payer-to-payee mapping and concentration

    • Contracts, invoices and updated expected activity

    • Counterparty links and adverse information

    5. Likely disposition

    Targeted information if the risk is stable and workflow permits; escalate if unavailable or inconsistent.

    6. Model verbal answer

    ““I would not close or automatically escalate on volume alone. The pattern could reflect legitimate growth and a subcontracting model, but the profile mismatch and pass-through behaviour are material. I would request the key contracts, invoices, counterparty relationships and updated KYC because those facts directly determine economic purpose. If they reconcile, closure may be supportable; if they do not, or if ownership and third-party relationships remain opaque, I would escalate.””

    7. Common mistakes to avoid

    • Requesting every document instead of decision-relevant documents

    • Calling rapid movement layering without considering the business model

    • Leaving the alert open indefinitely

    Case 3 — Student funnel account and rapid cross-border depletion

    1. Facts

    • Part-time student with stated income of $24,000 receives 94 e-transfers from unrelated people in five provinces over six weeks, totalling $146,000.

    • Funds are consolidated and wired within 24–48 hours to three foreign beneficiaries.

    • Less than 2% is retained. Memos are blank or generic.

    • Prior account use was rent, groceries and payroll.

    2. Main risks

    • Severe profile mismatch

    • Funnel/mule or unregistered-remittance pattern

    • Potential fraud victims or TF/sanctions exposure

    3. Questions Shazia should ask

    • Relationship to senders and beneficiaries?

    • Any shared devices, addresses or linked accounts?

    • Fraud complaints, recalls, prior UARs or intelligence?

    4. Additional information to review

    • Quantify sender geography, timing and destination concentration

    • Linked identifiers and related accounts

    • Source/use explanation and foreign-beneficiary screening

    5. Likely disposition

    Escalate for AML investigation; consider parallel fraud/specialist review.

    6. Model verbal answer

    ““I would escalate. The combination—not any single indicator—is decisive: the client profile does not support the volume, funds arrive from many unrelated and geographically dispersed senders, most leave internationally within 48 hours, and almost nothing is retained. A community collection is possible, but it is not supported by the known profile or transaction detail. I would direct the investigation to sender overlap, linked accounts, beneficiaries, potential fraud victims and any ML/TF or sanctions implications.””

    7. Common mistakes to avoid

    • Calling the student a money launderer

    • Ignoring a possible community-remittance explanation

    • Failing to give downstream direction

    Case 4 — Trade-finance pricing and third-party funding anomalies

    1. Facts

    • Canadian electronics importer pays $1.8 million for vaguely described “computer accessories” to a new supplier in a transshipment jurisdiction.

    • Unit prices appear several times higher than prior purchases and the route is indirect.

    • An unrelated company funded 40% of the payment.

    • Sales and available inventory data do not explain the purchase size.

    2. Main risks

    • Possible over-invoicing or value transfer

    • Opaque goods and route

    • Unexplained third-party funder

    • Sanctions/export-control overlap

    3. Questions Shazia should ask

    • What are the exact goods, quantity and market range?

    • What documents support shipment and customs entry?

    • What is the relationship with the funder and supplier?

    • Any ownership links or dual-use concerns?

    4. Additional information to review

    • Prior trade pattern and pricing

    • Invoices, bills of lading, packing/customs data and payment messages

    • Ownership, screening and specialist referrals

    5. Likely disposition

    Escalate for trade-based money-laundering review and any specialist assessment.

    6. Model verbal answer

    ““I would escalate. Pricing alone is not proof, but here it combines with vague goods, an indirect route, a purchase inconsistent with sales and substantial funding by an unrelated third party. The available information does not establish a credible commercial rationale. I would include the exact pricing and flow anomalies and direct the investigation to trade documents, party linkages, beneficial ownership and any sanctions or export-control concerns.””

    7. Common mistakes to avoid

    • Treating a price variance as proof

    • Pretending to be a customs or sanctions decision-maker

    • Ignoring legitimate bulk-purchase or financing explanations

    Case 5 — Correspondent bank with possible nested activity

    1. Facts

    • Respondent bank expected to serve retail customers in one region starts sending repeated USD payments from originators in several other jurisdictions.

    • Messages contain generic addresses and recurring intermediary institutions not disclosed in the relationship profile.

    • Several payments benefit unrelated Canadian entities with little apparent operational presence.

    • No confirmed sanctions match.

    2. Main risks

    • Activity inconsistent with expected markets

    • Possible nested correspondent relationships

    • Opaque payment information and shell-like beneficiaries

    3. Questions Shazia should ask

    • Is the respondent processing for other financial institutions?

    • Who are the underlying customers and beneficial owners?

    • Why did corridors and intermediaries change?

    • What is the purpose of the Canadian beneficiaries?

    4. Additional information to review

    • Respondent relationship profile and risk assessment

    • Corridor, intermediary and beneficiary concentration

    • Focused request through approved correspondent process

    5. Likely disposition

    Targeted respondent information if permitted and stable; escalate if opacity remains or policy requires.

    6. Model verbal answer

    ““I would treat this as a relationship-level and transaction-level concern. The new corridors, recurring undisclosed intermediaries and opaque Canadian beneficiaries are inconsistent with the expected respondent profile. I would seek focused information on nested relationships, underlying parties and purpose through the approved process. If the respondent cannot provide sufficient transparency, or policy requires immediate referral, I would escalate. The absence of a sanctions hit does not resolve the AML concern.””

    7. Common mistakes to avoid

    • Assuming all high-risk respondent activity is suspicious

    • Ignoring the correspondent relationship baseline

    • Asking the respondent broad, unfocused questions

    Case 6 — Human-trafficking indicators across linked accounts

    1. Facts

    • Three personal accounts receive frequent low-value e-transfers from many unrelated people with repeated initials and coded notes.

    • Funds are spent on hotels, ride-share, online advertising and prepaid products across several cities, then withdrawn in cash.

    • Account holders share addresses/devices and one is authorized on another account.

    • Activity spikes around a major entertainment event.

    2. Main risks

    • Coordinated network and possible control of vulnerable persons

    • Rapid depletion and cash conversion

    • Potential human-trafficking proceeds or facilitation

    3. Questions Shazia should ask

    • Are senders recurring across accounts?

    • What do occupations and KYC show?

    • What advertising/hotel merchants and locations are involved?

    • Any internal, fraud or law-enforcement information?

    4. Additional information to review

    • Cross-account network and sender overlap

    • Timing, geography, merchant categories and cash

    • Shared identifiers and prior referrals

    5. Likely disposition

    High-priority escalation with any appropriate live-risk/specialist pathway.

    6. Model verbal answer

    ““I would escalate on a high-priority basis. No single hotel or advertising transaction is determinative, but the linked control, numerous unrelated payers, coded references, event-related spike and rapid depletion through travel, advertising, prepaid products and cash create a coherent pattern not supported by ordinary personal use. I would avoid asserting trafficking as fact and direct the investigation to sender overlap, account control, merchant geography, prior intelligence and any vulnerable-person or live-risk response.””

    7. Common mistakes to avoid

    • Stating human trafficking as proven

    • Ignoring legitimate event-promotion activity

    • Failing to protect confidentiality and specialist boundaries

    Case 7 — Low-value terrorist-financing concern

    1. Facts

    • Ordinary payroll client makes 27 transfers of $200–$900 over four months to charities and individuals in a conflict-adjacent region.

    • Names vary slightly; several payments use the same foreign MSB.

    • Unrelated people fund the client shortly before some outgoing payments.

    • Open-source information raises unresolved concerns about one charity. Aggregate value is under $15,000.

    2. Main risks

    • Possible collection/fundraising

    • Identity ambiguity and conflict-region context

    • TF and sanctions/listed-entity overlap

    3. Questions Shazia should ask

    • Can beneficiary and charity identities be resolved?

    • What is the relationship and purpose?

    • Why is third-party funding present?

    • Is the fundraising model legitimate and documented?

    4. Additional information to review

    • Map incoming funds to outgoing payments

    • Resolve names/charity and screen through specialist process

    • Review prior pattern, memos and known client ties

    5. Likely disposition

    Escalate despite modest aggregate value.

    6. Model verbal answer

    ““I would escalate. Value is relevant to context, but there is no minimum amount below which TF risk disappears. The repeated transfers, third-party funding immediately beforehand, common MSB, name variation and unresolved charity information collectively warrant deeper review. I would preserve the legitimate humanitarian explanation and avoid treating the open-source concern as confirmed, while directing specialist identity and sanctions/listing checks.””

    7. Common mistakes to avoid

    • Dismissing the case because amounts are small

    • Treating a conflict region as proof

    • Calling an unresolved charity match confirmed

    Case 8 — Watchlist false positive versus potential sanctions evasion

    1. Facts

    • Corporate client attempts to pay a supplier whose name resembles a listed entity; transliteration is common.

    • Address and registration country differ, but a director alias and date of birth partially align.

    • Payment instructions recently changed to a new intermediary and the invoice says “industrial parts.”

    • Client presses for urgent release.

    2. Main risks

    • Potential true match or ownership/control concern

    • Intermediary change and opaque goods

    • Sanctions-evasion and trade risk

    3. Questions Shazia should ask

    • Full identifiers and corporate ownership?

    • Exact goods, end user and route?

    • Why did intermediary instructions change?

    • Any link between supplier and listed person/entity?

    4. Additional information to review

    • Immediate sanctions specialist process

    • Identity and ownership/control resolution

    • Trade/payment facts without tipping off

    5. Likely disposition

    Do not routine-close; refer immediately to sanctions control and assess AML escalation based on transaction facts.

    6. Model verbal answer

    ““I would separate the sanctions match from the AML decision. The different address and country support a possible false positive, but the partial director identifiers, intermediary change and opaque goods mean the match is not resolved. I would preserve and refer the attempted transaction through the sanctions process without tipping off. If identity, ownership or deceptive routing facts support possible evasion, I would also escalate for AML investigation.””

    7. Common mistakes to avoid

    • Releasing because the client is urgent

    • Calling a common-name hit a confirmed match

    • Conflating listed-property reporting and STR obligations

    Case 9 — Seasonal restaurant cash spike

    1. Facts

    • Established restaurant in a tourist area deposits cash 65% above its annual average for two summer months.

    • Deposits align with weekend events and point-of-sale sales; payroll, supplier payments and taxes also increase.

    • Cash is deposited at the usual branch and not immediately wired out.

    • KYC identifies seasonal business.

    2. Main risks

    • Cash-intensive business

    • Magnitude above baseline

    3. Questions Shazia should ask

    • Do POS, tax and expense patterns reconcile?

    • Are denominations/locations or deposit behaviour unusual?

    • Any unrelated third-party or international movement?

    4. Additional information to review

    • Compare same season in prior years

    • Sales, expenses, cash-to-card mix and retained balance

    • Deposit timing/location and adverse information

    5. Likely disposition

    Likely close if the seasonality and revenue evidence reconcile.

    6. Model verbal answer

    ““I would likely close. The cash increase is unusual against the annual average but consistent with the documented seasonal business, event timing, POS sales and corresponding increases in payroll, suppliers and tax payments. I would compare the same period in prior years and confirm no structuring, unusual locations or rapid onward wires. The closure rationale should explain the business evidence, not simply say the client is a restaurant.””

    7. Common mistakes to avoid

    • Escalating any cash spike

    • Ignoring cash denominations/locations

    • Closing without validating revenues and expenses

    Case 10 — Executive receives high-value asset-sale proceeds

    1. Facts

    • Long-standing executive receives a $2.4 million domestic wire from a law firm and sends $900,000 to an investment dealer.

    • The alert triggers on unusual value and rapid onward movement.

    • KYC and prior notes document real-estate ownership and substantial wealth.

    • Available sale/closing information identifies the property and law firm trust account.

    2. Main risks

    • High value and rapid movement

    • Potential third-party law firm

    3. Questions Shazia should ask

    • Do closing dates, amounts and parties reconcile?

    • Is the investment account in the client’s name?

    • Any unexplained residual transfers?

    4. Additional information to review

    • Source documentation and account ownership

    • Historical wealth/profile and related transactions

    • Screen parties and prior alerts

    5. Likely disposition

    Likely close if the sale and investment destination are corroborated.

    6. Model verbal answer

    ““I would likely close because the source and use of funds are transparent, fit the client’s profile and financial capacity, and are supported by closing information. The rapid movement is expected when sale proceeds are reinvested. I would verify the law-firm trust source and investment-account ownership and check for unexplained third-party dispersals. High value alone is not a reason to escalate.””

    7. Common mistakes to avoid

    • Giving a high-value client lighter scrutiny

    • Closing solely because a law firm is involved

    • Ignoring beneficial ownership of the destination

    Case 11 — Retiree sends repeated cryptocurrency payments after scam contact

    1. Facts

    • Retired client with limited digital history sends six wires totalling $180,000 to a crypto exchange over three weeks.

    • Branch notes say a “broker” coached the client and urged secrecy.

    • Funds came from liquidation of GICs; client is attempting another transfer.

    • No prior crypto activity.

    2. Main risks

    • Active investment scam and vulnerable-client harm

    • Potential mule/crypto laundering downstream

    • Immediate loss

    3. Questions Shazia should ask

    • Is the exchange account in the client’s name?

    • Any remote-access or account-takeover indicators?

    • What did the client say and who instructed them?

    • Can the next payment be controlled under policy?

    4. Additional information to review

    • Urgent fraud/vulnerable-person workflow

    • Transaction timeline and counterparties

    • AML assessment of beneficiaries and potential recovery

    5. Likely disposition

    Immediate fraud/live-risk referral and likely AML escalation depending on facts.

    6. Model verbal answer

    ““My first priority is the live client-harm risk. The sudden liquidation, new crypto activity, coaching and secrecy are strong scam indicators, and another payment is pending. I would immediately engage the authorized fraud or vulnerable-client control process and document the facts. In parallel, I would assess whether the movement and beneficiaries warrant AML escalation. I would not delay control action to complete a full AML review.””

    7. Common mistakes to avoid

    • Treating the retiree as the criminal actor

    • Waiting for proof before raising live risk

    • Ignoring AML implications because fraud is primary

    Case 12 — Repeat alert previously closed, but the pattern has changed

    1. Facts

    • A small import company had two prior international-wire alerts closed because suppliers and invoices were documented.

    • The new alert involves the same corridor but three new payers funding the account, new intermediary banks and payments to an entity sharing an address with the client’s owner.

    • Volume doubled; invoices are generic.

    2. Main risks

    • Prior closure may anchor the reviewer

    • New third-party and related-party activity

    • Trade and ownership opacity

    3. Questions Shazia should ask

    • What exactly was established in the prior closures?

    • Why did funding, intermediaries and counterparties change?

    • Are entities related through ownership/control?

    4. Additional information to review

    • Read prior rationales but independently assess new facts

    • Ownership/address links and trade documents

    • Updated KYC and flow comparison

    5. Likely disposition

    Likely escalate or seek narrow information only if it can promptly resolve the new pattern.

    6. Model verbal answer

    ““I would not let the prior closure decide the new alert. I would identify which facts remain the same and which changed. The new third-party funders, intermediaries, related address, generic invoices and doubled volume materially alter the risk. Unless targeted documents immediately reconcile those changes, I would escalate and explain why the prior legitimate pattern no longer resolves the current activity.””

    7. Common mistakes to avoid

    • Auto-closing because prior alerts were closed

    • Treating prior closure as evidence of wrongdoing

    • Failing to compare old and new facts explicitly

    Case 13 — International student tuition and living-cost inflows

    1. Facts

    • International student receives $58,000 over a semester from parents and a family business abroad.

    • Pays university tuition, rent and ordinary expenses; retains a reasonable balance.

    • One transfer routes through a family-owned company rather than a parent.

    • KYC documents family support but not the company.

    2. Main risks

    • Third-party company funding

    • International corridor and amount

    3. Questions Shazia should ask

    • Do parents own/control the company?

    • Does the amount fit tuition and living costs?

    • Any rapid unrelated transfers or cash?

    4. Additional information to review

    • Family/company relationship and source

    • University and expense payments

    • Historical pattern and screening

    5. Likely disposition

    Likely close after confirming the company relationship and source.

    6. Model verbal answer

    ““I would likely close after a focused verification of the family company. The amounts, timing and use of funds align with tuition and living expenses, and there is no pass-through behaviour. The corporate-originated transfer is the key gap; if the parents own the business and the funding is consistent with their support, the activity is reasonably explained. An unrelated company, unexplained onward wires or inconsistent source would change the decision.””

    7. Common mistakes to avoid

    • Treating all corporate funding of students as suspicious

    • Ignoring whether the amount fits expenses

    • Relying on nationality or corridor

    Case 14 — Commercial treasury account with high velocity but clear purpose

    1. Facts

    • Large Canadian company receives daily sales settlements and sweeps funds to a central treasury account overnight.

    • Balances are low at day-end and volumes are high.

    • The structure is documented in cash-management agreements and consistent with the corporate group’s history.

    • Counterparties are operating subsidiaries.

    2. Main risks

    • Rapid pass-through and low retained balance

    • High value

    3. Questions Shazia should ask

    • Are subsidiaries and treasury relationships verified?

    • Any new unrelated counterparties, corridors or deviations?

    • Does the sweep agreement match observed timing and accounts?

    4. Additional information to review

    • Group ownership and cash-management documentation

    • Historical pattern and new exceptions

    • Sanctions/adverse information as relevant

    5. Likely disposition

    Close if observed flows match the documented treasury model.

    6. Model verbal answer

    ““I would close if the corporate relationships and sweep arrangements are verified. High velocity and low retained balance can be normal in a documented treasury structure. I would focus on exceptions—unrelated parties, new corridors, amounts outside the agreement or unexplained manual transfers. The rationale should explain the economic model so the closure is defensible.””

    7. Common mistakes to avoid

    • Calling any pass-through account suspicious

    • Ignoring exceptions within a legitimate model

    • Closing because the company is large

    Case 15 — Charity with subcontractor payments and incomplete beneficial ownership

    1. Facts

    • Canadian charity receives donations from many individuals and sends 40% of funds to two overseas service providers.

    • Contracts exist but one service provider’s beneficial owners cannot be confirmed and invoices are high-level.

    2. Main risks

    • What services, end beneficiaries and local partners are documented?

    4. Additional information to review
