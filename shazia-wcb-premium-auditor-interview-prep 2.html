The posting describes responsibilities such as reviewing records for assessable earnings, reviewing employer operations for classification, verifying worker status and earnings for claims charging, investigating unregistered employers and unauthorized deductions, communicating findings/root causes/corrective actions, and educating employers on WCB legislation, pricing programs, and disability management.

5. Knowledge-based prep section

5.1 The core WCB premium audit concept

A WCB premium audit is a review of an employer’s operations and financial records to verify that the employer is compliant. WCB’s policy describes premium audits as formal examinations of employer operations and financial records, used to ensure employers pay premiums under the correct industry classification, report correct assessable earnings, and verify injured worker earnings.

The WCB employer fact sheet says audits may verify whether assessable earnings are reported correctly, optional coverage is in place as desired, business activities are in the proper industry classification, appropriate earnings and claims history are used to set rates, and claims are charged to the right account.

Interview wording Shazia can use:

“A premium audit supports fairness in the workers’ compensation system. It verifies that employers are reporting the right earnings, classified in the correct industry, and paying the appropriate premium. It also identifies root causes of reporting errors and gives employers guidance to correct issues going forward.”

5.2 Records a Premium Auditor may review

WCB’s premium audit fact sheet lists examples of records such as financial statements, payroll records, T4s, T4As and summaries, tax returns, general ledgers and accounts payable ledgers.

She should say:

“I would expect to reconcile across multiple sources rather than relying on one report: payroll registers, T4/T4A summaries, financial statements, general ledger wage accounts, accounts payable records for contractors, tax filings, and any documentation supporting exclusions or excess earnings.”

Bridge to her resume:

“That is similar to how I review transaction alerts: I don’t rely on one data point; I look at the full record, supporting documents, client profile, transactional activity, and escalation history before reaching a conclusion.”

5.3 Assessable earnings

Key facts:

  • Premiums are based on assessable earnings, subject to a maximum assessable earnings amount per worker. For 2026, WCB’s reporting fact sheet lists the maximum assessable earnings amount as $110,900. Earnings above the maximum are not premiumed, but are reported as excess earnings.
  • WCB’s fact sheet says items listed on T4 statements are generally assessable and gives guidance that the list is not all-inclusive.
  • Director earnings are often mistakenly included; WCB’s fact sheet says a company’s own directors’ earnings are not assessable/reportable unless optional personal coverage rules apply. Working shareholders who are not registered directors generally must be included.

Simple example she can use:

“If a worker earned $120,000 in 2026, I would not automatically assess the full $120,000. I would apply the 2026 maximum assessable earnings cap of $110,900 and treat the remaining $9,100 as excess earnings, assuming the earnings are otherwise assessable.”

If using the 2026 average rate of $1.46 per $100 of assessable earnings, the premium on $110,900 would be:

$110,900 ÷ 100 × $1.46 = $1,619.14

But she should add:

“The actual employer rate would depend on the employer’s rate group and experience, so I would not quote the average rate as the employer’s specific rate.”

5.4 Industry classification

WCB says industry classifications set the stage for rate setting. Employers are classified with other employers that have similar businesses and risks, not simply by the occupations of individual employees; rates vary by the loss experience of each group.

Interview wording:

“Classification is about the employer’s business operations and risk profile, not just job titles. For example, administrative staff in a construction company may still be connected to the construction operation for classification purposes, depending on the account structure. I would verify actual operations through records, interviews, invoices, website/business descriptions, and revenue or payroll allocation.”

5.5 Premium rates and experience rating

WCB’s 2026 rate-setting page says the average employer premium rate is $1.46 per $100 of assessable earnings, but individual rates vary based on the employer’s performance and rate group.

The 2026 pricing guide explains that claim costs and assessable earnings are reviewed over a historical experience period. For 2026 rates, the guide shows the experience period using 2022–2024, with a lag year before the rate year. It also explains that discount/surcharge calculations involve elements such as experience ratio, participation factor and eligibility factor.

Interview wording:

“Premiums are not arbitrary. They are linked to assessable earnings, industry classification, rate group performance, and the employer’s own claims experience where applicable. Accurate audit work matters because incorrect earnings, classification, or claims charging can affect the fairness of the employer’s rate and the overall funding of the system.”

5.6 Contractors, subcontractors, worker status and clearance letters

WCB says a company hiring contractors or subcontractors may be responsible for coverage and premiums if the contractor does not have or may not be eligible for a WCB-Alberta account. WCB encourages clearance letters before work starts and before payment, because a clearance letter confirms coverage, industry and good standing. The contractor page also explains that if a contractor does not meet the business test and is not eligible for a WCB account, they may be considered a worker of the principal and earnings must be reported.

Interview wording:

“For contractors, I would not assume they are independent just because they invoice. I would look at WCB coverage status, clearance letters, business test considerations, invoices, contracts, payment records, nature of work, and whether a deeming order or principal responsibility applies. The goal is to determine whether earnings should be included and whether the claim risk belongs to the principal account.”

5.7 Disability management, 72-hour reporting and modified work

Premium Auditors may also educate employers on disability management. WCB’s premium audit fact sheet says premium auditors work with select employers to improve disability management by focusing on 72-hour injury reporting performance and modified work percentage.

Employers are required to submit the employer report of injury within 72 hours after becoming aware of an injury or illness when it meets WCB reporting criteria, including lost time, modified work beyond the date of accident, treatment beyond first aid, medical aid expenses, death or permanent disability.

WCB’s modified work guidance says modified work helps workers return at the earliest safe opportunity, supports recovery, keeps workers connected to employment, and can reduce claim costs and premiums.

Interview wording:

“The audit role is not only retrospective. There is also a prevention and education element. If an employer has late injury reporting or weak modified-work practices, I would explain how early reporting and suitable modified duties support worker recovery and can reduce claim costs over time.”

5.8 Review and appeal awareness

If an employer disagrees with a premium-related decision, WCB’s premium review process encourages the employer to first contact the decision-maker, then request a written review through the Dispute Resolution and Decision Review Body, and then appeal a DRDRB decision to the independent Appeals Commission if still unresolved. Requests for review generally must be submitted within one year of the original decision.

Interview wording:

“If an employer disagreed with my finding, I would first listen and explain the evidence and policy considerations. If they provide new information, I would consider it fairly. If disagreement remains, I would explain the review process without becoming defensive.”

6. Knowledge-based interview questions and model answers

Q1. “What do you understand this role to be?”

Model answer:

“The Premium Auditor role helps ensure fairness and compliance in the workers’ compensation system. The auditor reviews employer records and operations to verify assessable earnings, correct industry classification, appropriate premium rate information, worker status, earnings for claims charging, and potential non-compliance such as unregistered employers or unauthorized deductions. The role also involves communicating findings, explaining root causes, recommending corrective actions, and educating employers on WCB legislation, pricing and disability management.”

Q2. “How would you approach a premium audit from start to finish?”

Model answer:

“I would use a structured approach: first understand the audit scope, employer account, industry classification and risk areas. Then request and review records such as payroll, T4/T4A summaries, financial statements, GL accounts, AP ledgers and tax filings. I would reconcile reported assessable earnings to source records, test exceptions, review contractor payments and worker status, verify operations for classification, and identify any claims charging or earnings issues.

I would document my work clearly, discuss preliminary findings with the employer, consider additional evidence, finalize conclusions, communicate the root cause, and provide corrective actions so the employer can report accurately going forward.”

Q3. “What records would you request?”

Model answer:

“At minimum, I would expect payroll registers, T4/T4A slips and summaries, financial statements, general ledger wage accounts, accounts payable ledgers, contractor invoices, tax returns, corporate/director information, worker lists, and any supporting documentation for exclusions, excess earnings, optional personal coverage or classification allocation. I would tailor the request to the employer’s operations and audit scope.”

Q4. “What are assessable earnings?”

Model answer:

“Assessable earnings are the earnings used to calculate WCB premiums, subject to WCB rules and the annual maximum assessable earnings cap. For 2026, the maximum assessable earnings amount is $110,900 per worker. I would verify gross earnings, taxable benefits, T4/T4A reporting, directors/shareholders treatment, excess earnings, and any allocation across industries or jurisdictions.”

Q5. “How are premium rates set?”

Model answer:

“At a high level, premiums are based on assessable earnings multiplied by the applicable rate per $100 of earnings. The rate is influenced by the employer’s industry classification, rate group and experience. The 2026 average employer rate is $1.46 per $100, but each employer’s rate may differ depending on performance and rate group. Accurate audit work matters because incorrect earnings, classification or claims information can affect the fairness of premium rates.”

Q6. “What is industry classification and why does it matter?”

Model answer:

“Industry classification groups employers with similar business activities and risks. It matters because premium rates are tied to the loss experience and risk of the classification or rate group. I would focus on actual business operations, not just job titles or what the employer calls itself. I would verify through interviews, invoices, websites, revenue sources, payroll allocation and operational records.”

Q7. “How would you handle contractors?”

Model answer:

“I would not assume an invoice means the person is outside WCB reporting. I would review clearance letters, whether the contractor has their own WCB account, whether they meet the business test, contract terms, nature of work, payment records and whether a deeming order or principal responsibility applies. If the contractor is considered a worker of the principal, earnings may need to be reported and any claim may be charged to the principal.”

Q8. “What is the connection between premium auditing and disability management?”

Model answer:

“Premium auditing verifies historical reporting and classification, but it can also help employers improve future outcomes. WCB focuses on timely injury reporting and modified work because early reporting and suitable return-to-work options can reduce claim duration, support recovery and help control costs. So the auditor’s communication role includes education, not just adjustments.”

Q9. “How would your AML background help in this role?”

Model answer:

“AML investigations require disciplined review of complex records, pattern recognition, understanding client profiles, identifying anomalies, documenting evidence and escalating appropriately. Premium audit uses a different subject matter, but the method is similar: compare what is reported to source records, identify discrepancies, verify explanations, apply policy, document findings, and communicate professionally.”

Q10. “What would you do if you were unsure about a WCB policy?”

Model answer:

“I would not guess. I would explain that I need to verify the policy, consult the current WCB policy manual or internal guidance, speak with my supervisor or subject matter expert if needed, and follow up with a clear answer. Accuracy and consistency are more important than giving an immediate but uncertain answer.”

Q11. “How do you ensure audit quality?”

Model answer:

“I use a structured workpaper approach: define the scope, list records reviewed, document reconciliations, identify exceptions, record employer explanations, tie conclusions to evidence and policy, and maintain a clear audit trail. I also separate facts from assumptions and seek peer or supervisor review when a finding is complex or high-impact.”

Q12. “What Excel skills are relevant here?”

Model answer:

“I would expect to use Excel for reconciliations, pivot tables, filters, sorting, duplicate identification, conditional formatting, SUMIFS, XLOOKUP or VLOOKUP, exception testing, sampling, and creating clear schedules that tie payroll, T4/T4A summaries, GL wage accounts, contractor payments and reported WCB earnings together.”

7. Scenario-based prep section

For scenario questions, Shazia should use this structure:

1. Clarify the issue.
2. Gather evidence.
3. Apply policy/legislation.
4. Communicate respectfully.
5. Document everything.
6. Escalate when required.
7. Educate and provide corrective action.

A strong Premium Auditor answer is rarely “I would catch them.” It is usually:

“I would verify, document, explain, correct, and educate, while remaining fair and objective.”

Scenario 1: Employer is defensive and says, “Why are you auditing us? We did nothing wrong.”

What they test: Customer service, fairness, communication, de-escalation.

Strong answer:

“I would acknowledge their concern and explain that being selected for a premium audit does not automatically mean wrongdoing. I would explain the purpose: to verify earnings, classification, coverage and claims charging so premiums are fair and accurate. I would set expectations about the process, records required, timelines and confidentiality. I would keep the tone collaborative, but also clear that WCB has a compliance responsibility. My goal would be to make the process transparent and reduce defensiveness.”

Shazia’s bridge:

“In AML investigations, people may not always understand why information is being requested. I’ve learned to explain the purpose, stay professional, and focus on facts rather than assumptions.”

Scenario 2: Employer provides incomplete records.

What they test: Persistence, audit judgment, documentation, escalation.

Strong answer:

“First, I would identify exactly what is missing and why it matters to the audit. I would provide a clear written request with due dates and offer to clarify acceptable formats. If the employer has system limitations, I would work with them to obtain equivalent records. If records remain incomplete, I would document all requests, assess what can be concluded from available information, consult my supervisor, and follow WCB procedures for non-compliance. I would avoid making unsupported assumptions, but I would also not close the audit without sufficient evidence.”

Scenario 3: Reported WCB earnings are $4 million, but T4/T4A summaries and payroll records show $5.2 million.

What they test: Technical audit thinking.

Strong answer:

“I would treat that as a reconciliation issue, not immediately as wrongdoing. I would compare the annual return to payroll registers, T4/T4A summaries, GL wage accounts and any supporting schedules. Then I would identify legitimate reconciling items such as excess earnings above the maximum, non-assessable director earnings, interprovincial allocations, multiple classifications, or other non-assessable amounts. I would also review whether contractors or shareholder wages were excluded incorrectly. Once the variance is explained, I would document the schedule, discuss it with the employer, and determine whether an adjustment is required.”

Scenario 4: Employer says all subcontractors are independent and therefore excluded.

What they test: Worker status, contractors, policy awareness.

Strong answer:

“I would not accept or reject that statement at face value. I would request contractor lists, invoices, contracts, clearance letters and proof of WCB account status. I would look at whether each contractor meets the business test, whether they have active WCB coverage, whether a deeming order exists, and whether the principal may be responsible for coverage. If the evidence shows some contractors should be treated as workers or deemed workers, I would explain the basis and include those earnings as required.”

Scenario 5: Employer’s business operations do not match the classification on the account.

What they test: Classification judgment and investigation.

Strong answer:

“I would verify actual operations through multiple sources: employer interview, website, invoices, revenue breakdown, payroll by activity, job descriptions, contracts, and maybe a site visit if appropriate. Since classification is based on business operations and risk, not just job titles, I would compare the evidence to WCB classification guidance. If a change appears required, I would document the evidence, consult internally if needed, explain the impact to the employer, and provide guidance on future reporting.”

Scenario 6: You find payroll deductions that appear to charge workers for WCB coverage.

What they test: Compliance seriousness, professionalism, evidence.

Strong answer:

“I would treat that as a serious compliance issue but remain factual. I would gather evidence such as paystubs, payroll deduction codes, payroll journals and employer explanations. I would verify whether the deduction is actually related to WCB premiums or something else. If it is an unauthorized premium deduction, I would explain that WCB rules prohibit passing the cost of WCB coverage to workers, document the finding, consult my supervisor, and follow WCB procedures for corrective action and any required escalation.”

WCB’s contractor guidance specifically reminds employers that it is illegal to deduct the cost of workers’ compensation from workers, contractors and subcontractors.

Scenario 7: You receive an allegation of an unregistered employer.

What they test: Investigation discipline and fairness.

Strong answer:

“I would start by gathering objective information: business identity, operations, location, workers, dates of operation, industry, public records, invoices or advertisements if available, and any complainant information. I would not assume the allegation is true. I would contact the employer professionally, explain the purpose, gather facts, and determine whether registration is required. If they should be registered, I would follow WCB procedures to bring the account into compliance and assess premiums appropriately.”

Scenario 8: Employer strongly disagrees with your audit findings.

What they test: Conflict management and review awareness.

Strong answer:

“I would listen carefully and ask them to identify the specific part they disagree with. I would walk through the evidence and policy basis for the finding in plain language. If they provide new documentation, I would review it objectively and adjust if appropriate. If disagreement remains, I would explain the available review process and document the discussion. I would not personalize the disagreement or become defensive.”

Scenario 9: Employer has multiple industries and administration staff cannot be clearly allocated.

What they test: Understanding of allocation/proration.

Strong answer:

“I would first determine whether the employer has one classification or multiple classifications. If there are multiple industries and administration earnings cannot be directly allocated, I would look for WCB guidance on prorating those earnings across classifications. I would request payroll by department, revenue by activity or other allocation support. If direct allocation is not possible, I would apply the appropriate proration method consistently and document the rationale.”

Scenario 10: Worker claim appears charged to the wrong account.

What they test: Claims charging and cross-functional work.

Strong answer:

“I would verify the worker’s employer at the time of injury, worker status, payroll records, account structure, contractor relationship if any, and the business activity involved. I would compare the evidence to WCB account and claims information. If it appears incorrectly charged, I would document the evidence and coordinate with the appropriate internal team because claims charging can affect experience rating and premium fairness.”

Scenario 11: Employer asks, “How can we reduce our premiums?”

What they test: Employer education without overpromising.

Strong answer:

“I would explain that premiums are influenced by assessable earnings, classification, rate group and claims experience, so I cannot promise a specific reduction. But I can educate them on practices that may improve performance over time: accurate reporting, timely injury reporting, effective modified work, safety programs, and WCB resources such as return-to-work planning, OIS or PIR where applicable. I would keep the advice factual and within WCB guidance.”

WCB’s “lower your premiums” guidance says employers with good safety performance and return-to-work programs can earn lower rates, and it identifies early reporting, PIR, OIS, safety associations and modified work as relevant tools.

Scenario 12: You discover an error in your own audit work.

What they test: Integrity, accountability, trust.

Strong answer:

“I would immediately assess the impact of the error, inform my supervisor, correct the workpaper and any affected schedule, and communicate any revised conclusion to the employer if needed. I would document what changed and why. I would also identify how to prevent recurrence, such as adding a reconciliation step or peer review checkpoint. Trust is built by owning mistakes and correcting them promptly.”

This answer aligns strongly with WCB’s value of trust, which includes being consistent, reliable, delivering on commitments and owning mistakes.

Scenario 13: You are working from home, travelling occasionally, and managing several audits at once.

What they test: Independence, organization, reliability.

Strong answer:

“I would manage audits through a clear tracking system: deadlines, records requested, employer follow-ups, analysis status, supervisor review dates and final reporting dates. I would prioritize based on risk, statutory or internal deadlines, and employer responsiveness. I would communicate early if a timeline is at risk. I am comfortable working independently, but I also know when to escalate or ask for guidance.”

Scenario 14: Employer asks a technical question and you do not know the answer.

What they test: Judgment and honesty.

Strong answer:

“I would not guess. I would say, ‘That’s a good question and I want to make sure I give you the correct answer. I’m going to verify the current policy and follow up.’ Then I would consult WCB policy or my supervisor and provide a clear response. In compliance work, accuracy is more important than sounding certain in the moment.”

Scenario 15: A colleague disagrees with your interpretation.

What they test: Teamwork and openness.

Strong answer:

“I would ask them to walk me through their reasoning and compare both interpretations to policy and evidence. If needed, I would involve a supervisor or subject matter expert. I would be open to changing my view if their evidence is stronger. The goal is a correct and consistent decision, not proving I was right.”

Scenario 16: You receive messy payroll data in Excel.

What they test: Practical data handling.

Strong answer:

“I would first preserve the original file and work from a copy. Then I would clean the data: standardize dates, employee IDs and pay codes; remove duplicates only after confirming; use pivot tables to summarize by employee, pay code and period; reconcile totals to payroll registers and T4 summaries; identify outliers such as earnings above the maximum, unusual deductions, missing employees or contractor payments; and document every transformation so the audit trail is clear.”

Scenario 17: You suspect deliberate underreporting.

What they test: Professional skepticism and restraint.

Strong answer:

“I would avoid making accusations. I would gather evidence, identify the discrepancy, ask for explanations, test those explanations against source documents, and document facts carefully. If the evidence suggests intentional non-compliance, I would consult my supervisor and follow WCB procedures for escalation. My role is to be thorough, fair and evidence-based.”

8. Behavioral STAR story bank for Shazia

She should prepare six real stories from her experience. Each story should be sanitized; she must not disclose confidential client, bank or investigation details.

Use this formula:

Situation: What was happening?
Task: What was your responsibility?
Action: What specific steps did you take?
Result: What changed or what decision was supported?
Transfer: How does this apply to WCB premium audit?

Story 1: Complex investigation / high-risk network

Use for questions like:
“Tell us about a complex file.”
“Tell us about a time you analyzed large amounts of information.”
“Tell us about a time you had to make a risk-based decision.”

Model structure:

“In my financial crime role, I was assigned a high-risk case involving complex transaction activity and a network of related parties. My task was to determine whether the activity was consistent with the client profile or whether it required escalation. I reviewed transaction monitoring alerts, account activity, KYC information, due diligence documentation and fund-flow patterns. I identified anomalies, documented the rationale, collaborated with internal teams for missing information, and prepared a clear report for decision-makers.

The result was a well-supported compliance decision and, where appropriate, escalation or internal control action. The relevance to WCB is that premium audit also requires analyzing records, finding inconsistencies, verifying explanations and documenting conclusions in a way others can rely on.”

Story 2: Unusual Activity Report investigation

Use for:
“Tell us about regulatory compliance.”
“How do you handle escalations?”
“How do you assess whether something is suspicious?”

Model structure:

“A branch or internal department submitted an unusual activity report. I reviewed the concern, compared it to account activity and client profile, gathered supporting information, and determined whether the concern met escalation or reporting thresholds. I documented my rationale clearly and ensured the conclusion aligned with policy.”

Transfer line:

“For WCB, the equivalent is receiving an allegation or audit red flag and approaching it objectively: verify, gather records, apply policy, document and communicate.”

Story 3: Fund-flow analysis

Use for:
“Tell us about your analytical skills.”
“How do you identify anomalies?”

Model structure:

“I had to establish the pattern of funds moving through accounts. I reviewed transaction histories, grouped activity by source and destination, looked for unusual volumes or counterparties, and compared the pattern to the expected client profile. I documented anomalies and supported the final risk assessment.”

Transfer line:

“In premium audit, I would use the same pattern-recognition skill to reconcile payroll, GL wage accounts, T4/T4A summaries, contractor payments and WCB reported earnings.”

Story 4: Cross-functional document gathering

Use for:
“Tell us about working with others.”
“Tell us about incomplete information.”

Model structure:

“In a case review, I needed KYC and due diligence documents from different departments. I identified exactly what was required, contacted the appropriate teams, explained the urgency and purpose, tracked outstanding items, and used the documents to complete the analysis.”

Transfer line:

“Premium auditing will require similar coordination with employers and internal WCB teams to obtain records and resolve gaps.”

Story 5: Internal control referral / risk mitigation

Use for:
“Tell us about a time you improved controls.”

12. Technical mini-cheat sheet Shazia should memorize

Premium audit purpose

Verify correct assessable earnings, classification, rate-related information, worker status/earnings, claims charging, and compliance.

Premium formula concept

Premium = assessable earnings ÷ 100 × employer premium rate

2026 maximum assessable earnings

$110,900 per worker.

2026 average employer rate

$1.46 per $100 of assessable earnings, but individual employer rates vary.

Classification principle

Based on actual business operations and similar risk, not only employee job titles.

Audit records

Payroll records, T4/T4A and summaries, financial statements, tax returns, general ledger, accounts payable, contractor invoices, clearance letters.

Contractors

Check WCB account status, clearance letters, business test, contracts, invoices, work performed, deeming orders and principal responsibility.

Disability management

Timely reporting, modified work, return-to-work planning and safety performance can affect claim outcomes and premiums.

Employer injury reporting

Employers report qualifying injuries within 72 hours after becoming aware.

Review process

Contact decision-maker, request review through DRDRB, appeal DRDRB decision to Appeals Commission if still unresolved.

She should practice explaining this reconciliation:

Scenario:
Employer reported $950,000 to WCB. Payroll register shows $1,030,000. T4 summary shows $1,025,000. One director earned $80,000. One worker earned $120,000 in 2026.

How to analyze:

  1. Start with payroll/T4 totals.
  2. Identify reconciling difference between payroll and T4.
  3. Remove non-assessable director earnings if applicable.
  4. Apply 2026 MAE cap to worker earning $120,000:
    • assessable portion = $110,900
    • excess = $9,100
  5. Check if any contractors, shareholder wages, interprovincial work, benefits or non-assessable items explain remaining variance.
  6. Compare final assessable earnings to WCB-reported $950,000.
  7. Document adjustment and ask employer for support.
